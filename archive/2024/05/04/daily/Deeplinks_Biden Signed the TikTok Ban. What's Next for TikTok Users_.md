Over the last month, lawmakers moved swiftly to pass legislation that would effectively ban TikTok in the United States, eventually including it [in a foreign aid package](https://www.congress.gov/bill/118th-congress/house-bill/815/text?s=3&r=4&q=%7B%22search%22%3A%22aid%22%7D#H49326F77434C4336B3BA44830FF4A94A) that was signed by President Biden. The impact of this legislation isn’t entirely clear yet, but what is clear: whether TikTok is banned or sold to new owners, millions of people in the U.S. will no longer be able to get information and communicate with each other as they presently do.

#### **What Happens Next?**

At the moment, TikTok isn’t “banned.” The law gives ByteDance 270 days to divest TikTok before the ban would take effect, which would be on January 19th, 2025. In the meantime, we expect courts to determine that the bill is unconstitutional. Though there is no lawsuit yet, one on behalf of TikTok itself [is imminent](https://twitter.com/TikTokPolicy/status/1783148312914268213).

There are three possible outcomes. If the law is struck down, [as it should be](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2024/03/congress-should-give-unconstitutional-tiktok-bans), nothing will change. If ByteDance divests TikTok by selling it, then the platform would still likely be usable. However, there’s no telling whether the app’s new owners would change its functionality, its algorithms, or other aspects of the company.
