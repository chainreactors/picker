In the wake of rolling out its own proposal to significantly limit a key law protecting internet users’ speech in the summer of 2020, the Department of Justice under the first Trump administration actively worked with lawmakers to support further efforts to stifle online speech.

The new [documents](https://www.documentcloud.org/documents/26050394-05-30-25-oip-final-response/), disclosed in an EFF Freedom of Information Act (FOIA) lawsuit, show officials were [talking](https://www.documentcloud.org/documents/26050394-05-30-25-oip-final-response/#document/p339) with Senate staffers [working to pass](https://www.documentcloud.org/documents/26050394-05-30-25-oip-final-response/#document/p29) speech- and privacy-chilling bills like the [EARN IT Act](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2022/02/its-back-senators-want-earn-it-bill-scan-all-online-messages) and [PACT Act](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2021/03/even-changes-revised-pact-act-will-lead-more-online-censorship) (neither became law). DOJ officials also [communicated](https://www.documentcloud.org/documents/26050394-05-30-25-oip-final-response/#document/p410) with an organization that sought to condition Section 230’s legal protections on websites using age-verification systems if they hosted sexual content.
