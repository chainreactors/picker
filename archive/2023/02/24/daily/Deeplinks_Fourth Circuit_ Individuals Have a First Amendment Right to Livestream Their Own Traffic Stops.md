In a partial victory for police accountability, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Fourth Circuit [held](https://scholar.google.com/scholar_case?case=6065516506570982252) that the First Amendment protects a passenger who livestreams the traffic stop of the car he is traveling in. EFF filed an [amicus brief in *Sharpe v. Winterville*](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2021/12/eff-federal-appeals-courts-hold-police-accountable-violating-civilians-right-0) in 2021 in support of the plaintiff. Unfortunately, the Fourth Circuit’s opinion is not a total win for First Amendment rights because the court curtailed the plaintiff’s ability to hold the individual officers accountable.

After police officers tased, choked, and severely beat [Dijon Sharpe](https://pacer-documents.s3.amazonaws.com/199/21-01827/00408650104.pdf) during a traffic stop, he decided that next time he was in a car that was pulled over, he would livestream and record the encounter.
