The amount of data modern cars collect is a serious privacy concern for all of us. But in an abusive situation, tracking can be a [nightmare](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2022/12/fighting-tech-enabled-abuse-2022-year-review).

As a [New York Times article](https://www.nytimes.com/2023/12/31/technology/car-trackers-gps-abuse.html) outlined, modern cars are often connected to apps that show a user a wide range of information about a vehicle, including real-time location data, footage from cameras showing the inside and outside of the car, and sometimes the ability to control the vehicle remotely from their mobile device. These features can be useful, but abusers often turn these conveniences into tools to harass and control their victimsâ€”or even to locate or spy on them once they've fled their abusers.

California is currently considering three bills intended to help domestic abuse survivors endangered by vehicle tracking. Unfortunately, despite the concerns of advocates who work directly on [tech-enabled abuse](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2022/12/fighting-tech-enabled-abuse-2022-year-review), these proposals are moving in the wrong direction.
