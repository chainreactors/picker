A [companion bill](https://castor.house.gov/uploadedfiles/kosa_language.pdf) to the Kids Online Safety Act (KOSA) was introduced in the House last month. Despite minor changes, it suffers from the same fundamental flaws as its [Senate counterpart](https://www.congress.gov/118/bills/s1409/BILLS-118s1409rs.pdf). At its core, this bill is still an unconstitutional censorship bill that restricts protected online speech and gives the government the power to target services and content it finds objectionable. Here, we break down why the House version of KOSA is just as dangerous as the Senate version, and why it’s crucial to continue opposing it.

### **Core First Amendment Problems Persist**

EFF has [consistently](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2023/08/congress-amended-kosa-its-still-censorship-bill) [opposed](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2024/02/dont-fall-latest-changes-dangerous-kids-online-safety-act) KOSA because, through several iterations of the Senate bill, it continues to open the door to government control over what speech content can be shared and accessed online. Our [concern](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2024/03/analyzing-kosas-constitutional-problems-depth), which we share with others, is that the bill’s broad and vague provisions will force platforms to censor legally protected content and impose age-verification requirements. The age verification requirements will drive away both minors and adults who either lack the proper ID, or who value their privacy and anonymity.
