When Congress eventually reopens, the 2026 [National Defense Authorization Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/2296/text) (NDAA) will be moving toward a vote. This gives us a chance to see the priorities of the Secretary of Defense and his Congressional allies when it comes to the military—and one of those priorities is buying technology, [especially AI](https://www.nextgov.com/acquisition/2025/07/pentagon-awards-multiple-companies-200m-contracts-ai-tools/406698/), with [less of an obligation to prove it’s effective and worth the money](https://www.lawfaremedia.org/article/how-acquisition-reform-could-make-military-ai-more-expensive-and-less-safe) the government will be paying for it.

As reported by [Lawfare](https://www.lawfaremedia.org/article/how-acquisition-reform-could-make-military-ai-more-expensive-and-less-safe), “This year’s defense policy bill—the National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA)—would roll back data disclosures that help the department understand the real costs of what they are buying, and testing requirements that establish whether what contractors promise is technically feasible or even suited to its needs.” This change comes amid a push from the Secretary of Defense to “[Maximize Lethality](https://media.defense.gov/2025/Mar/07/2003662943/-1/-1/1/DIRECTING-MODERN-SOFTWARE-ACQUISITION-TO-MAXIMIZE-LETHALITY.PDF)” by acquiring modern software “at a speed and scale for our Warfight...
