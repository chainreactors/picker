Turkey’s government recently passed a new law aimed at curbing disinformation that citizens have dubbed the “censorship law,” [according to reports](https://bianet.org/english/media/268138-disinformation-law-final-stage-of-akp-s-media-restrictions). The new law was met with [condemnation](https://freeturkeyjournalists.ipi.media/turkey-international-delegation-condemns-passage-of-disinformation-law-and-issues-call-to-protect-safety-and-freedom-of-journalists-ahead-of-2023-elections/) from both inside the country and abroad.

Troublingly, the vaguely-worded law, passed by parliament on October 13, prescribes three years’ imprisonment for anyone who publishes “false information” with the intent to “instigate fear or panic” or “endanger the country’s security, public order and general health of society.”

This latest law is one of many attempts by the country to restrict its citizens’ internet usage. Dubbed an “[enemy of the internet](https://rsf.org/en/turkey)” by Reporters Without Borders several times, Turkey’s government [censors thousands of websites](https://freedomhouse.org/country/turkey/freedom-net/2022) and frequently shows up on social media companies’ transparency reports for demanding content removals. The country is also among the world’s [top jailers of journalists](https://cpj.org/reports/2018/12/journalists-jailed-imprisoned-turkey-china-egypt-saudi-arabia/).
