At times this year, it seemed that Congress was going to give up its duty to protect our rights online—particularly when the Senate passed the dangerous Kids Online Safety Act (KOSA) by a large majority in July. But this legislation, which would chill protected speech and almost certainly result in privacy-invasive age verification requirements for many users to access social media sites, did not pass the House this year, thanks to strong opposition from EFF supporters and others.

KOSA, [first introduced in 2022](https://first%20introduced%20in%202022/), would allow the Federal Trade Commission to sue apps and websites that don’t take measures to restrict young people’s access to content. Congress introduced a number of versions of the bill this year, and we [analyzed](https://analyzed/) [each](https://each/) of them. Unfortunately, the threat of this legislation still looms over us as we head into 2025, especially now that the bill has passed the Senate. And [just a few weeks ago](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2024/12/xs-last-minute-update-kids-online-safety-act-still-fails-protect-kids-or-adults), its authors introduced an amended version to respond to criticisms from some House members.
