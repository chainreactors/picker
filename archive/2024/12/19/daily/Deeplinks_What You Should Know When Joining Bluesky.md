Bluesky promises to rethink social media by focusing on openness and user control. But what does this actually mean for the millions of people joining the site?

November was a good month for alternatives to X. Many users hit their balking point after two years of [controversial changes](https://techcrunch.com/2024/06/05/elon-musk-twitter-everything-you-need-to-know/) turned Twitter into X, a [restrictive](https://www.reuters.com/technology/twitter-now-needs-users-sign-view-tweets-2023-06-30/) hub filled with [misinformation](https://www.reuters.com/world/us/wrong-claims-by-musk-us-election-got-2-billion-views-x-2024-report-says-2024-11-04/) and [hate speech](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/article/2024/sep/05/racism-misogyny-lies-how-did-x-become-so-full-of-hatred-and-is-it-ethical-to-keep-using-it). Musk’s involvement in the U.S. presidential election was the last straw for many who are now looking for greener pastures.

Threads, the largest alternative, grew about 15% with [35 million new users](https://thehill.com/policy/technology/5010118-instagrams-threads-gets-35-million-new-users-this-month/). However, the most explosive growth came from Bluesky, seeing over [500% growth](https://www.npr.org/2024/11/19/g-s1-34898/bluesky-traffic-surge-after-election) and a total user base of over [25 million](https://bsky.jazco.dev/stats) users at the time of writing.
