The mandatory retention of [metadata](https://ssd.eff.org/module/why-metadata-matters) is an evergreen of European digital policy. Despite a number of rulings by Europe’s highest court, [confirming](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/en/TXT/?uri=CELEX:62012CJ0293) [again](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/en/TXT/?uri=CELEX:62015CA0203) [and](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:62018CJ0511) [again](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/en/TXT/?uri=CELEX:62020CJ0140) [the incompatibility](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/en/TXT/?uri=CELEX:62019CA0793) of general and indiscriminate data retention mandates with European fundamental rights, the European Commission is taking major steps towards the re-introduction of EU-wide data retention mandates. Recently, the Commission launched a Call for Evidence on data retention for criminal investigations—the first formal step towards a legislative proposal.

The European Commission and EU Member States have been attempting to revive data retention for years. For this purpose, a secretive “[High Level Group on Access to Data for Effective Law Enforcement](https://home-affairs.ec.europa.eu/networks/high-level-group-hlg-access-data-effective-law-enforcement_en)” has been formed, usually referred to as High level Group (HLG) “Going dark”.
