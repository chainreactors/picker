*In the past few years, governments across the world have*[*rolled out different digital identification*](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2024/09/digital-id-isnt-everybody-and-thats-okay) *options, and now there are efforts encouraging online companies to implement identity and age verification requirements with digital ID in mind. This blog is the second in a short series that explains digital ID and the pending use case of age verification. Upcoming posts will evaluate what real protections we can implement with current digital ID frameworks and discuss how better privacy and controls can keep people safer online.*

Digital identity encompasses various aspects of an individual's identity that are presented and verified through either the internet or in person. This could mean a digital credential issued by a certification body or a mobile driver’s license provisioned to someone’s mobile wallet. They can be presented in plain text on a device, as a scannable QR code, or through tapping your device to something called a Near Field Communication (NFC) reader. There are other ways to present credential information that is [a little more privacy preserving](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2025/07/zero-knowledge-proofs-alone-are-not-digital-id-solution-protecting-user-privacy), but in practice those three methods are how we are seeing digital ID being used today.
