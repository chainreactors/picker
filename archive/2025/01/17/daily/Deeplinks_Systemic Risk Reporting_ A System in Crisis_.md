* [Polski](/deeplinks/2025/01/systemic-risk-reporting-system-crisis?language=pl)

The first batch of [reports assessing the so called “systemic risks”](https://dsa-observatory.eu/2024/12/09/dsa-risk-assessment-reports-are-in-a-guide-to-the-first-rollout-and-whats-next/) posed by the largest online platforms are in. These reports are a result of the [Digital Services Act (DSA)](https://www.eff.org/issues/eu-policy-principles), Europe’s new law regulating platforms like Google, Meta, Amazon or X, and have been eagerly awaited by civil society groups across the globe. In their reports, companies are supposed to assess whether their services contribute to a wide range of barely defined risks. These go beyond the dissemination of illegal content and include vaguely defined categories such as negative effects on the integrity of elections, impediments to the exercise of fundamental rights or undermining of civic discourse. We have previously warned that the subjectivity of these categories invites a [politization of the DSA.](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2024/08/eff-and-partners-breton-uphold-fundamental-rights-enforcing-dsa)

In view of a [new DSA investigation](https://ec.europa.eu/commission/presscorner/detail/en/ip_24_6487) into [TikTok’s potential role](https://www.euronews.com/next/2024/12/23/romanian-centre-right-partys-tiktok-campaign-used-for-far-right-win-report) in [Romania’s presidential election](https://www.ft.com/content/74f6623b-8ed1-4b6e-a037-1516ae84283e), we take a look at the reports and the framework that has produced them to understand their value and limitations.
