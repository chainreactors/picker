![US flag with spying eyes for stars](https://www.eff.org/files/styles/resized_banner/public/banner_library/flag-surveillance-color.jpg?itok=RdvIKri2)

We've seen months of continued public confirmation that [Americans’ privacy is being violated by surveillance under Section 702](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2023/07/deja-vu-fbi-proves-again-it-cant-be-trusted-section-702), and widespread criticism from civil society, activists, surveillance-[skeptical bipartisan congressional committees](https://rollcall.com/2023/07/11/fbi-director-to-face-harsh-critics-at-house-judiciary-oversight-hearing/), and even the [overly timid](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2023/04/congressional-hearing-pclob-members-suggest-bare-minimum-702-reforms) Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board. Now, a separate  group of White House-appointed experts just [flinched](https://www.reuters.com/world/us/white-house-recommends-scaling-back-fbi-authority-under-intelligence-act-2023-07-31/) on renewing the law without reforms. It’s good to see even the White House [signal](https://www.whitehouse.gov/briefing-room/statements-releases/2023/07/31/statement-from-national-security-advisor-jake-sullivan-and-principal-deputy-national-security-advisor-jon-finer-on-the-presidents-intelligence-advisory-boards-review-of-section-702-o/) that improvements are needed, but it was immediately undermined by the tininess of their proposed changes.
