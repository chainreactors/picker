![An illustration of a police car mounted with cameras scanning an SUV.](https://www.eff.org/files/styles/resized_banner/public/banner_library/og-policealpr-2.png?itok=ijXwK8eO)

Flock Safety loves to crow about the thousands of local law enforcement agencies around the United States that have adopted its [avian](https://www.flocksafety.com/articles/product-announcement-q2-2023)-[themed](https://swshoa.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/12/Flock-Safety.pdf) [automated license plate readers](https://www.eff.org/cases/automated-license-plate-readers) (ALPRs). But when a privacy activist launched a website to map out the exact locations of these pole-mounted devices, the company tried to clip his wings.

The company sent [DeFlock.me](https://deflock.me/) and its creator [Will Freeman](https://www.404media.co/the-open-source-project-deflock-is-mapping-license-plate-surveillance-cameras-all-over-the-world/) a [cease-and-desist letter](https://www.eff.org/document/flock-safety-cd-deflockme), claiming that the project dilutes its trademark. Suffice it to say, and to lean into ornithological wordplay, the letter is birdcage liner.

Representing Freeman, EFF sent Flock Safety [a letter rejecting the demand](https://www.eff.org/document/eff-deflock-letter-flock-safety), pointing out that the grassroots project is well within its First Amendment rights.
