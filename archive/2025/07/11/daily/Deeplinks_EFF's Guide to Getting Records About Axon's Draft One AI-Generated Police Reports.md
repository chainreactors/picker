The moment Axon Enterprise announced a new product, Draft One, that would allow law enforcement officers to use artificial intelligence to automatically generate incident report narratives based on body-worn camera audio, everyone in the police accountability community immediately started asking the same [questions](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2024/05/what-can-go-wrong-when-police-use-ai-write-reports).

What do AI-generated police reports look like? What kind of paper trail does this system leave? How do we get a hold of documentation using public records laws?

Unfortunately, obtaining these records isn't easy. In many cases, it's straight-up impossible.

**Read our full report on how Axon's Draft One defies transparency expectations by design [here](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2025/07/axons-draft-one-designed-defy-transparency).**

In some jurisdictions, the documents are walled off behind government-created barriers. For example, California fully exempts police narrative reports from public disclosure, while other states charge fees to access individual reports that become astronomical if you want to analyze the output in bulk.
