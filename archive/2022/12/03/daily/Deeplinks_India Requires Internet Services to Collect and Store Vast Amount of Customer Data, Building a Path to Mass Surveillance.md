Privacy and online free expression are once again under threat in India, thanks to vaguely worded cybersecurity directions—promulgated by India’s [Computer Emergency Response Team](https://www.cert-in.org.in/PDF/CERT-In_Directions_70B_28.04.2022.pdf) (CERT-In) earlier this year—that impose draconian [mass surveillance obligations](https://internetfreedom.in/cert-in-guidelines-on-cybersecurity-an-explainer/) on internet services, threatening privacy and anonymity and [weakening security online](https://www.accessnow.org/cms/assets/uploads/2022/06/India-CERT-in-Directions-joint-letter_1-June-2022.pdf).

[Directions 20(3)/2022 - CERT-In](https://www.cert-in.org.in/PDF/CERT-In_Directions_70B_28.04.2022.pdf) came into effect on June 28th, sixty days [after being published](https://www.cert-in.org.in/PDF/CERT-In_directions_extension_MSMEs_and_validation_27.06.2022.pdf) [without stakeholder consultation](https://www.cert-in.org.in/PDF/CERT-In_Directions_70B_28.04.2022.pdf). Astonishingly, India’s Minister of State for Electronics and Information Technology (MeitY) Rajeev Chandrasekhar [said](https://www.moneycontrol.com/news/business/rajeev-chandrasekhar-fires-salvo-at-vpns-follow-the-rules-or-leave-india-8530631.html) the government wasn’t required to get public input because the directions have “no effect on citizens.” The Direction [itself](https://www.cert-in.org.in/PDF/CERT-In_Directions_70B_28.04.2022....
