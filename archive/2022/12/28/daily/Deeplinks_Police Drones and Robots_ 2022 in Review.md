The rising tide of policing by robots and drones may seem relentless or even inevitable. But activism, legislative advocacy, and public outrage can do a lot to protect our safety and freedom from these technologies.

This year began with a [report](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2022/01/how-are-police-using-drones) that elucidated what police are doing with drones. Answer? Not much for now. A law in Minnesota mandates police departments report  all of the times they deployed drones and for what reason. We’ve suspected that police have few clear uses, other than invasive surveillance. The Minnesota report reveals that drones were mostly  just for training purposes.

One purpose Axon was hoping to find for drones this year was to stop school shooters. The company announced they were developing a drone that came with a mounted taser for the purpose of subduing people in dangerous situations. The backlash was immediate. [After a majority of Axon’s ethics board resigned the company paused the project](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2022/06/your-resistance-pauses-axons-dangerous-drone-tasers).

In Oakland and in San Francisco, activists defeated municipal plans to authorize police to use deadly force with remote-controlled robots. In Oakland, police hoped to use a [shotgun-mounted robot](https://theintercept.com/2022/10/17/police-robot-gun-oakland/)-–a plan which received so much backlash the proposal was pulled in just a few days.
