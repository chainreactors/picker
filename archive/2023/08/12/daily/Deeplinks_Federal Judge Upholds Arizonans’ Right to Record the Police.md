The Arizona legislature last year [passed a law](https://www.azcentral.com/story/news/politics/legislature/2022/07/06/recording-police-close-range-now-illegal-new-bill-signed/7816271001/) ([H.B. 2319](https://www.azleg.gov/legtext/55leg/2R/bills/HB2319S.pdf) codified at [A.R.S. § 13-3732](https://www.azleg.gov/viewdocument/?docName=https://www.azleg.gov/ars/13/03732.htm)) banning the video recording of police activity within eight feet of officers, making doing so a [class 3 misdemeanor](https://www.azleg.gov/ars/13/00707.htm) (which would allow for up to 30 days in jail). The law included some exceptions, such as for “a person who is the subject of police contact.”

A coalition of news organizations and the ACLU of Arizona [sued state and county government officials](https://www.azcentral.com/story/news/local/arizona/2022/08/23/republic-others-challenge-arizona-law-banning-recordings-police/7867538001/) in federal court arguing that the law was unconstitutional. EFF filed an [amicus brief](https://www.eff.org/press/releases/arizona-law-tramples-peoples-constitutional-right-record-police) in support of the plaintiffs in the district court.
