* [Deutsch](/deeplinks/2024/10/germany-rushes-expand-biometric-surveillance?language=de)

Germany is a leader in privacy and data protection, with many Germans being [particularly sensitive](https://digitalcourage.de/blog/2021/history-german-privacy-movement) to the processing of their personal data – owing to the country’s totalitarian history and the role of surveillance in both Nazi Germany and East Germany.

So, it is disappointing that the German government is trying to push through Parliament, at record speed, a “[security package](https://www.dw.com/en/berlin-unveils-new-security-package-after-solingen-attack/a-70082878)” that would increase biometric surveillance at an unprecedented scale. The proposed measures contravene the government’s own [coalition agreement](https://www.bundesregierung.de/breg-en/federal-government/coalition-agreement-482268), and undermine European law and the German constitution.

In response to a [knife-stabbing](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/crlr2z23ykno) in the West-German town of Solingen in late-August, the government has introduced a so-called “[security package](https://www.heise.de/en/news/Facial-recognition-harsh-criticism-for-all-round-biometric-surveillance-9853123.html)” consisting of a bouquet of measures to tighten asylum rules and introduce new powers for law enforcement authorities.

Among them, three stand out due to their possibly disastrous effect on fundamental rights online.
