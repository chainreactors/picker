For those living in one of the [22 states](https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2024/us/abortion-laws-roe-v-wade.html) where abortion is banned or heavily restricted, the internet can be a lifeline. It has essential information on where and how to access care, links to abortion funds, and guidance on ways to navigate potential legal risks. Activists use the internet to organize and build community, and reproductive healthcare organizations rely on it to provide valuable information and connect with people in need.

But both Republicans and Democrats in Congress are now actively pushing for federal legislation that could cut youth off from these vital healthcare resources and stifle online abortion information for adults and kids alike.

This summer, the U.S. Senate passed the [Kids Online Safety Act (KOSA)](https://www.congress.gov/bill/118th-congress/senate-bill/2073/text), a bill that would grant the federal government and state attorneys general the power to [restrict online speech they find objectionable](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2022/11/kosa-would-let-government-control-what-young-people-see-online) in a misguided and ineffective attempt to protect kids online. A number of organizations have already sounded the alarm on [KOSA’s danger to online LGBTQ+ content](https://www.them.us/story/kids-online-safety-act-kosa-youth-lgbtq-content), but the hazards of the bill don’t stop there.
